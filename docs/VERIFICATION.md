# Adversarial verification of load-bearing design claims

(Generated by the dov-modem-design research workflow. Each claim was attacked by an independent fact-checker.)

## Claim 1 — verdict: partially-supported

**Claim:** GSM/AMR/EFR narrowband voice vocoders apply VAD/DTX/comfort-noise that will gate or replace steady or silence-like signals, so a data-over-voice modem must make every symbol resemble continuous ACTIVE voiced speech to avoid being suppressed.

**Reasoning:** The claim is correct on the core mechanism but overstated in two technically important ways, so it is partially supported rather than fully supported.

WHAT IS CORRECT (supported):
- GSM FR, EFR, and AMR narrowband codecs do implement the VAD -> DTX -> comfort-noise (CN/SID) chain. A Voice Activity Detector decides per 20 ms frame whether the signal is active; during detected inactivity the encoder stops sending normal speech frames and instead sends low-bitrate Silence Descriptor (SID) frames, and the far end synthesizes comfort noise rather than reproducing the original waveform (3GPP/ETSI TS 26.092 Comfort Noise Aspects, TS 26.093 Source Controlled Rate, TS 26.094 VAD; ETS 300 729 for EFR DTX; RFC 4867). So 'silence-like' or low-energy/stationary input genuinely gets replaced by comfort noise. The data-over-voice modem literature confirms that VAD/DTX 'well suited to voice... can strongly degrade other types of signals such as data modulated signals,' which is why these modems use 'speech-like (SL) symbol' codebooks designed to be classified as active. This validates the general thrust of the claim.

WHAT IS WRONG / OVERSTATED (refuting nuance):
1. 'Voiced' is too narrow. The VAD flags ACTIVE SPEECH, not specifically VOICED speech. The ETSI/AMR VAD specification states the VAD's function is to indicate frames containing 'signals that should be transmitted, i.e. speech, music or information tones.' Active speech explicitly includes UNVOICED sounds (e.g. fricative /s/), and DTX hangover (~40-300 ms / seven extra frames in EFR) is specifically designed to keep quiet trailing UNVOICED sounds classified as active. Correspondingly, the modems use a mix of speech-like symbols drawn from observed human speech, not exclusively voiced/pitched phonemes. The symbol must read as 'active and speech/tone/music-like,' which is broader than 'voiced.'

2. 'Steady or tone signals will be gated' is partly wrong. The VAD contains an explicit TONE-detection mechanism (the 'tone flag'). In EFR/AMR the VAD detects 'information tones' (e.g. one or two sinusoids) via short-term prediction gain and deliberately classifies them as ACTIVE so they are NOT suppressed by DTX. So a steady pure tone is not necessarily treated as silence; it can be kept active. (Caveat: in some specs the tone flag is evaluated only in the downlink VAD.) The dominant problem for steady/tone signals through a vocoder is therefore not primarily DTX suppression but DISTORTION: the CELP/ACELP analysis-by-synthesis model re-synthesizes the waveform and badly mangles non-speech signals even when they are transmitted as active. The claim conflates 'suppressed by DTX' with the broader 'destroyed by the vocoder,' and the latter is the larger reason symbols must be speech-like.

3. 'Will gate' is conditional, not guaranteed. DTX is configured by the network per cell, independently for uplink and downlink, and can be switched off. When DTX is off, there is no gating/comfort-noise substitution at all (though VAD-driven rate adaptation and vocoder distortion still apply). EFR DTX in particular is optional. So the suppression is conditional on network configuration.

NET: The underlying engineering intuition (make symbols look like active, high-energy, speech-like signals so VAD keeps them active and DTX/CN does not replace them) is sound and is exactly what real data-over-voice modems do. But the specific wording 'continuous ACTIVE voiced speech' is inaccurate: (a) unvoiced and tonal/music signals are also kept active, so 'voiced' is wrong; (b) steady tones are not automatically gated — the VAD has explicit tone handling to keep them active; and (c) gating is contingent on network DTX configuration. A more accurate statement is that symbols must be classified as ACTIVE speech-like signals (voiced, unvoiced, or tonal) AND survive the vocoder's lossy CELP re-synthesis, with DTX/CN being only one of the suppression/distortion hazards.

**Corrected:** GSM FR/EFR/AMR narrowband vocoders use VAD-driven DTX and comfort-noise generation that, when enabled by the network, replace signals the VAD classifies as inactive (low-energy/stationary, silence-like) with low-rate SID frames and synthesized comfort noise. A data-over-voice modem must therefore make symbols be classified as ACTIVE by the VAD and survive the codec's lossy CELP/ACELP re-synthesis — meaning they must be high-energy, speech-/tone-/music-like signals. They do NOT have to be specifically 'voiced': unvoiced (noise-like) and tonal symbols also pass the VAD as active (the VAD has explicit tone detection and unvoiced/hangover handling). Moreover, vocoder distortion of non-speech waveforms — not just DTX suppression — is a primary reason symbols must resemble speech, and DTX gating is contingent on per-cell, per-direction network configuration (and is optional, e.g., for EFR).

## Claim 2 — verdict: partially-supported

**Claim:** Coherent phase modulation (PSK/QAM) does NOT survive CELP/RPE-LTP voice vocoders because they do not preserve waveform phase or absolute amplitude; robust DoV signaling must be frequency/energy/shape-based (FSK, multi-tone, or speech-like symbol dictionaries) with non-coherent or correlation/matched-filter demodulation.

**Reasoning:** The claim's underlying mechanism and its constructive prescription are well supported by the data-over-voice (DoV) literature, but its two absolute words — "coherent" and "must" — overstate the case in ways the evidence partially refutes.

WHAT IS SOLIDLY SUPPORTED:
1) Vocoders break absolute phase. CELP/ACELP/VSELP and RPE-LTP (GSM full-rate) are analysis-by-synthesis / parametric coders that transmit LPC + excitation parameters, not the waveform; the decoder re-synthesizes a perceptually-similar but not phase-identical waveform. Sarafin/Tomic's ARDMA paper states this explicitly: "Due to the specific property of the frame-based GSM speech coding system, which does not assure arbitrary phase-shifting preservation, the coherent, as well as phase-based modulation techniques cannot be utilized," citing "asynchronous windowing of the input speech or modulation signal inside the RPE-LTP, ACELP, or VSELP." It adds that GSM transcoding has "a strong influence on phase, amplitude, and spectral characteristics."
2) Coherent PSK in particular fails. Hermes (Dhananjay et al., ACM CoNEXT 2010) tests ASK/FSK/PSK/OFDM through real voice codecs and concludes: "Even very small changes in phase are sufficient to cause discontinuities ... they get severely distorted by the codec ... this renders PSK impossible to realize over memoryful codecs." PSK demodulation also needs reliable symbol-boundary/phase reference, which the codec destroys ("virtually impossible to perform time synchronization").
3) Absolute amplitude is unreliable. Hermes: due to AGC and per-hop gain changes, "we should therefore not rely on the amplitude of the output waveforms"; VAD can also delete non-speech-like signals. The QAM-over-AMR-WB study confirms that for higher-order constellations (M=8,16, which encode amplitude), "the amplitude of signals passed through the vocoder is drastically changed, therefore high distortions are expected."
4) The prescribed remedy is exactly what the field uses. Robust DoV systems are frequency/shape/energy based with non-coherent/correlation detection: Hermes' final scheme (IncDec) is a constant-differential FSK detected by FFT + closest-neighbor (energy-in-frequency-bin) matching; the speech-like-symbol modems (Kondoz/Katugampala lineage) and ARDMA synthesize voiced-speech-like symbols decoded by AR/LSF analysis + weighted-Euclidean (minimum-distance) matching against a codebook; Mashhadi/Boloursaz et al. model the vocoder as a Discrete Memoryless Channel and use codebook/sphere-packing symbols with minimum-distance/ML detection — all non-coherent, correlation/matched-filter-like. M-FSK is a recurring baseline. This is a strong consensus.

WHERE THE CLAIM OVERSTATES (why "partially"):
A) "Coherent" is the load-bearing qualifier. What dies is coherent phase tracking. Phase information is not categorically unusable: differential PSK (DPSK) is repeatedly cited as the standard workaround precisely because it "eliminates problems caused by random phase drifts." DPSK is a phase modulation, decoded non-coherently. So a stricter true statement is "coherent PSK/QAM fails; differential/non-coherent phase signaling can be made to work." The claim already says demodulation should be non-coherent, but its blanket header "phase modulation (PSK/QAM) does not survive" is imprecise — non-coherent (differential) phase does have a foothold.
B) A genuine counterexample to the absolute wording exists. Ciornei, Bogdan, Scripcariu et al. (2014), "Sensitivity analysis of QAM over AMR-WB," report working QAM through the AMR-WB codec at 1600 bps (SER 2.2e-5), 2000 bps (1.5e-4), 3200 bps (2.4e-3), 4000 bps (8e-3). However, this partially reinforces the mechanism: the well-performing low-order cases are M=2/M=4 (i.e., phase-only BPSK/QPSK), it is over wideband AMR-WB (a higher-rate codec that preserves more waveform structure than narrowband CELP/RPE-LTP), the results are codec-in-the-loop simulations (not necessarily real transcoded/tandem cellular links), and the amplitude-bearing high-order QAM is precisely where it breaks down. So "QAM survives" is true only in a narrow, low-order, high-rate-codec, idealized sense.
C) "Must" is too strong as stated. The correct framing is statistical/practical: frequency/shape/energy-based, non-coherently-detected signaling is what robustly survives narrowband CELP/RPE-LTP and tandem/transcoded paths; it is the dominant and recommended approach, not a logical necessity for every codec/condition.

NET: The claim is correct about the physics (no absolute phase, unreliable absolute amplitude) and correct about the recommended design (frequency/energy/shape symbols, non-coherent/correlation/matched-filter demodulation). It is overstated in asserting categorically that all PSK/QAM "does NOT survive" — coherent PSK/QAM indeed fails through CELP/RPE-LTP, but differential (non-coherent) phase keying works, and low-order phase constellations have been demonstrated over the higher-rate AMR-WB codec. Hence partially-supported.

**Corrected:** Coherent PSK/QAM that relies on an absolute phase reference and/or absolute amplitude does NOT survive CELP/ACELP/VSELP/RPE-LTP voice vocoders, because these analysis-by-synthesis parametric coders re-synthesize a perceptually-similar waveform without preserving arbitrary phase (asynchronous windowing) and apply AGC/VAD that corrupt absolute amplitude. Consequently, robust data-over-voice signaling is overwhelmingly frequency/energy/shape-based (M-FSK, multi-tone/OFDM-with-1-bit/subchannel, or speech-like / AR-LSF symbol dictionaries) detected non-coherently via correlation, FFT-energy, or minimum-distance/matched-filter methods. Note two caveats: (1) non-coherent differential phase keying (DPSK) is a documented workaround that does carry phase information, so phase is not categorically unusable — only coherent phase tracking fails; and (2) low-order, phase-only constellations (BPSK/QPSK) have been demonstrated over the higher-rate wideband AMR-WB codec in codec-in-the-loop tests, whereas amplitude-bearing high-order QAM (M>=8) reliably breaks down.

## Claim 3 — verdict: partially-supported

**Claim:** AMR-NB at 12.2 kbit/s (MR122) is essentially the same ACELP codec as GSM-EFR (GSM 06.60), so emulating EFR can be approximated with opencore-amrnb in MR122 mode.

**Reasoning:** I tried to refute this claim but the core of it holds up against authoritative sources, with one technical caveat that prevents a fully "supported" verdict.

WHAT IS CORRECT:
1) Both are 12.2 kbit/s ACELP coders operating on 8 kHz / 13-bit input, 20 ms (160-sample) frames. GSM-EFR (GSM/3GPP 06.60) uses ACELP at 12.2 kbit/s; AMR-NB's top mode MR122 is also 12.2 kbit/s ACELP. (Wikipedia EFR/AMR, Adaptive Digital, GeeksforGeeks.)
2) The 12.2 mode of AMR was literally adopted FROM GSM-EFR. RFC 3267 section 3.1 states verbatim: "the 12.2 kbps mode [is adopted] as GSM-EFR [12]." So this is not a coincidence of bitrate — they share the same speech-coding core by design.
3) The core compression bitstreams are compatible for speech frames. Multiple sources state "the core compression schemes of the AMR-12.2 speech coding scheme and the GSM-EFR speech coding scheme are bit stream compatible, at least for frames containing coded speech." The FreeCalypso gsm-codec-lib "AMR-EFR-philosophy" doc confirms: "In the absence of SIDs, EFR and MR122 are directly interoperable in that the output of an EFR encoder can be fed to the input of an AMR decoder, and vice-versa." 3GPP even formally permits "An alternative implementation of the Enhanced Full Rate speech service based on the 12.2 kbit/s mode of the Adaptive Multi Rate coder."
4) opencore-amrnb exposes an MR122 mode (12.2 kbps), so using it as an EFR approximation is technically reasonable for the speech path.

WHY ONLY "PARTIALLY-SUPPORTED" / WHERE IT FAILS:
1) NOT bit-exact, even for speech. The FreeCalypso authoritative implementation note is explicit: "the two codecs are NOT identical at the bit-exact level! The differences are subtle." So "essentially the same" is true at the algorithm/interoperability level but false at the bit-exact level — they are interoperable, not identical. The word "approximated" in the claim correctly hedges this.
2) DTX / SID / comfort-noise frames are NOT compatible and are not just a minor detail: "the format of SID frames and even the very paradigm of how DTX works are completely different between EFR and AMR." The CN spectrum/energy parameters are quantized differently; an EFR SID delta-quantizes LSPs and code gain over a 7-frame DTX hangover, whereas AMR uses SID_FIRST (notification only) and SID_UPDATE (absolutely quantized LSPs + frame energy). So if "emulating EFR" includes DTX/silence behavior, opencore-amrnb MR122 will NOT match EFR — it would need separate EFR-specific SID/DTX handling.
3) Practical packing/framing differs: EFR's 244-bit frame uses GSM channel-coding bit ordering; AMR/MR122 has its own payload formats (RFC 3267/4867). A raw MR122 bitstream is not drop-in identical to an EFR TRAU/over-the-air frame without reordering, though the underlying 244 speech bits map across.

NET: As an approximation of the EFR speech codec, opencore-amrnb in MR122 mode is a sound and standards-endorsed choice — the speech core is the same ACELP and is interoperable. But "essentially the same codec" overstates it for anything involving bit-exactness, DTX/comfort-noise, or frame framing, where the two genuinely differ. The claim's own qualifiers ("essentially," "approximated") are doing real work and are what keep it from being refuted.

**Corrected:** AMR-NB's 12.2 kbit/s mode (MR122) shares the same 12.2 kbit/s ACELP speech-coding core as GSM-EFR (GSM 06.60) — in fact AMR's 12.2 mode was adopted directly from GSM-EFR (RFC 3267), and for coded-speech frames the two bitstreams are interoperable (an EFR-encoded frame can be decoded by an AMR decoder and vice versa). However, they are NOT bit-exact identical even for speech frames, and their DTX/SID comfort-noise mechanisms are completely different. Therefore opencore-amrnb in MR122 mode is a reasonable APPROXIMATION of the EFR speech path, but it is not a drop-in bit-exact EFR replacement; faithful EFR emulation additionally requires EFR-specific SID/DTX handling and frame bit-ordering.

## Claim 4 — verdict: partially-supported

**Claim:** The most robust published approach for data over a GSM/AMR voice channel is a trained dictionary of short SPEECH-LIKE symbols matched to the vocoder, and realistic end-to-end DoV throughput over real networks is on the order of a few hundred bps up to ~2 kbps (not the ~9.6+ kbps of PSTN voiceband modems).

**Reasoning:** The claim has two parts; the evidence cuts differently on each.

PART 1 ("most robust = trained speech-like symbol dictionary"): REFUTED as stated. The trained speech-like / codebook symbol approach is a major, well-established line of work (LaDue et al., IEEE TVT 2008, genetic-algorithm-evolved symbols over GSM EFR; Boloursaz Mashhadi & Kazemi et al., IET Communications 2015-2016 and IEEE TVT 65(11):8964-8977, 2016, who model the vocoder as a Discrete Memoryless Channel and optimize a codebook to maximize its capacity; Sayadiyan/Shahbazi). But the strongest published evidence on ROBUSTNESS over real, heterogeneous networks actively argues AGAINST it. The peer-reviewed Hermes paper (Dhananjay et al., ACM MobiCom 2010) states that symbol dictionaries trained over a particular codec (e.g., EFR) 'might not work over any generic and unknown voice channel,' because cellular codecs (notably AMR, with 14 modes) switch mode/bitrate on the fly so the codec-matched symbol set cannot be fixed in advance. Hermes deliberately does NOT use a speech-like dictionary — it uses an adaptive, constant-differential FSK / narrowband modulation scheme with robust transcoding/FEC — and achieves 1.2 kbps at BER ~1e-5 (FER <1%) across multiple real US carriers (AT&T, T-Mobile, etc.). The most widely DEPLOYED real-network DoV system, Airbiquity's aqLink (in-band software modem in 6M+ devices over GSM/CDMA/UMTS voice), is likewise a proprietary in-band modem, not a published speech-like dictionary. So 'the most robust published approach is a speech-like dictionary' is not the consensus; for robustness over UNKNOWN/heterogeneous live channels the literature points to adaptive generic modulation (Hermes) instead. The speech-like dictionary is best characterized as the highest-RATE approach for a KNOWN, fixed codec, not the most robust over real networks.

PART 2 (throughput range): MOSTLY SUPPORTED in spirit, with the lower numbers, but the '~2 kbps' ceiling is too low for the published literature and the framing conflates 'real-network end-to-end' with 'simulation/controlled over-the-air.' Real-network end-to-end academic results: Hermes 1.2 kbps over live carriers; SMS-equivalent baseline 224 bps; aqLink real deployments commonly in the hundreds-of-bps range. These support 'a few hundred bps up to ~1.2 kbps' robustly over real networks. However, published speech-like/codebook results report higher rates: LaDue 4 kbps at 2.5% SER over EFR; Boloursaz/Kazemi report ~1.6 kbps (SER ~2e-5) up to 4 kbps (SER ~8e-3) over AMR-WB and 3-4 kbps over EFR via codebook/sphere-packing methods; a 'universal' technique reports up to ~6.4 kbps. Most of these higher figures are codec-simulation or controlled over-the-air rather than validated as robust over arbitrary live networks — which actually reinforces the claim's underlying point that REALISTIC robust throughput is well below voiceband-modem rates — but they contradict the literal '~2 kbps' upper bound on published throughput.

The PSTN contrast is CORRECT and well grounded: ~9.6 kbps for GSM corresponds to Circuit-Switched Data (a dedicated transparent data bearer), and V.32/V.34 voiceband modems achieve 9.6 kbps+ over the additive-noise PSTN channel; neither is data-over-voice. DoV must survive a memoryful, lossy, non-additive vocoder (CELP/ACELP) plus VAD/AGC, so it is fundamentally limited to roughly 0.2-4 kbps depending on codec knowledge and required error rate — far below 9.6 kbps. That qualitative gap is solidly supported.

NET: The throughput ballpark and the PSTN contrast are sound; the specific '~2 kbps' upper cap understates published rates (3-6.4 kbps in simulation/controlled tests); and the central characterization that the speech-like dictionary is THE most robust approach is contradicted by the leading real-network-validated work (Hermes), which abandons dictionaries precisely for robustness reasons. Hence partially-supported.

**Corrected:** For data over a GSM/AMR voice channel, the trained speech-like-symbol/codebook approach (matched to a KNOWN vocoder) is the highest-RATE published method, reporting roughly 3-4 kbps (up to ~6.4 kbps in some simulations) over EFR/AMR — but mostly under codec-simulation or controlled over-the-air conditions. For ROBUSTNESS over real, heterogeneous, unknown live networks, the leading peer-reviewed work (Hermes, MobiCom 2010) deliberately avoids codec-matched dictionaries in favor of adaptive generic modulation, achieving ~1.2 kbps at BER ~1e-5. Realistic robust end-to-end DoV throughput over real networks is on the order of a few hundred bps up to ~1-1.2 kbps (with up to ~3-4 kbps achievable when the codec is known/fixed) — far below the ~9.6 kbps of GSM Circuit-Switched Data or PSTN V.32/V.34 voiceband modems, because the vocoder is a memoryful, lossy, non-additive channel rather than an additive-noise one.

## Claim 5 — verdict: supported

**Claim:** On Debian, libgsm and libopencore-amrnb can be linked into a Rust program via hand-written extern FFI without -dev headers, using GNU ld exact-name linking such as passing -l:libgsm.so.1 when the unversioned .so symlink is absent.

**Reasoning:** I attempted to refute this claim and could not; every component holds, and I confirmed it end-to-end on a real Debian system (this machine: Debian, GNU ld 2.44, rustc 1.93.1).

1) Package layout matches the claim's premise. On Debian, the runtime packages ship ONLY versioned shared objects with no unversioned .so symlink: libgsm1 contains /usr/lib/x86_64-linux-gnu/libgsm.so.1 (-> libgsm.so.1.0.19), and libopencore-amrnb0 contains libopencore-amrnb.so.0 (-> libopencore-amrnb.so.0.0.5). The unversioned symlinks (libgsm.so, libopencore-amrnb.so) AND the headers (gsm.h, opencore-amrnb/interf_*.h) live exclusively in the -dev packages (libgsm1-dev, libopencore-amrnb-dev). Verified both from packages.debian.org file lists and from `dpkg -l`/`ls` on the live system, where the -dev packages are NOT installed and the unversioned symlinks are confirmed absent.

2) GNU ld exact-name linking is real and documented. The binutils ld manual states: "If namespec is of the form :filename, ld will search the library path for a file called filename" (rather than transforming to libnamespec.so/.a). So `-l:libgsm.so.1` requests that exact file, bypassing the standard lib<name>.so naming that requires the dev symlink. This is the precise mechanism the claim names.

3) Hand-written extern FFI without headers works. Rust `extern "C" { ... }` blocks let you declare foreign functions manually with no header and no bindgen (the compiler trusts the signatures; it is unsafe in the FFI sense). Confirmed by Rust std/reference docs.

4) End-to-end empirical test (the decisive evidence). With only the runtime packages installed (no -dev, no headers, no unversioned .so), I wrote a Rust program with hand-declared `gsm_create`/`gsm_destroy` (libgsm) and `Decoder_Interface_init`/`Decoder_Interface_exit` (libopencore-amrnb), compiled with `-C link-arg=-l:libgsm.so.1 -C link-arg=-l:libopencore-amrnb.so.0`. It linked (exit 0), produced NEEDED entries libgsm.so.1 and libopencore-amrnb.so.0, ran successfully, and both real library calls returned valid non-null handles. As a control, plain `-lgsm` and `#[link(name="gsm")]` BOTH failed with "unable to find library -lgsm" precisely because the unversioned symlink is absent — proving the `-l:` exact-name form is the necessary workaround, exactly as claimed.

Nuances/caveats (do not break the claim): (a) The default Rust attribute `#[link(name="gsm")]` and `cargo:rustc-link-lib=gsm` do NOT work here; you must inject the verbatim arg via `-C link-arg=-l:...` or build.rs `cargo:rustc-link-arg=-l:...`. The claim correctly specifies the `-l:` form. (b) "GNU ld" is slightly imprecise as to what actually ran: modern rustc on this Debian defaults to invoking LLVM's lld (`-fuse-ld=lld`), not GNU ld, for the final link. However, lld implements the identical `-l:filename` syntax (it succeeded), the syntax is a GNU ld feature by origin/documentation, and GNU ld is Debian's system default linker (/usr/bin/ld = GNU ld 2.44) which also supports it — so the claim works whether the actual linker is GNU ld or lld. (c) Runtime resolution works because the binary's NEEDED/SONAME is the versioned name (libgsm.so.1), which is exactly what the runtime package provides, so no dev symlink is needed at run time either. The program ran with exit 0.

Net: the claim is accurate and reproducible; the only refinement is that the exact-name link arg must be passed verbatim (not via the default link-lib mechanism), and the underlying linker may be lld rather than GNU ld, both of which support the syntax.

**Corrected:** On Debian, libgsm (libgsm1) and libopencore-amrnb (libopencore-amrnb0) can be linked into a Rust program via hand-written extern "C" FFI declarations with no -dev headers or bindgen, by passing the exact-name link argument (e.g. -C link-arg=-l:libgsm.so.1 / build.rs cargo:rustc-link-arg=-l:libopencore-amrnb.so.0) so the linker resolves the versioned .so directly when the unversioned .so symlink (dev-only) is absent. The exact-name `-l:filename` form is a GNU ld feature and is also supported by LLVM lld, which is what rustc invokes by default; it must be supplied as a verbatim linker arg, since the default #[link(name=...)] / rustc-link-lib mechanism still expects the unversioned symlink and fails without it.
